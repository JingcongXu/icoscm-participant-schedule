A047 Track 4 Lecture Room 1 13:00-14:00

Industry 4.0 Technologies Adoption for Sustainable and Resilient Agri-Food Supply Chains: An Integrated Decision-Making Approach

Presenting author
Asmit Dey
Corresponding author
Asmit Dey
Abstract

Agri-Food Supply Chains (AFSCs) are necessary for ensuring incessant supply of food products (Silva et al., 2025) and challenged by the uncertain disruptions caused due to geopolitical events, climate change, mismatch in supply and demand, natural calamities, etc. (Belhadi et al., 2024; Piancharoenwong and Badir, 2024). These complexities are further increased as AFSCs strive to follow sustainable practices. Thus, this study identifies the critical success factors (CSFs) with the help of a literature review and was followed by semi-structured interviews of professionals from both industry and academia facilitating the I4.0 Technologies (I4.0T) adoption for integrating resilience and sustainability in AFSCs. It proposes an extended TOE framework (TOE-R), considering “resilience” as a fourth dimension, and prioritize the identified CSFs by employing the Fuzzy-Evidential Reasoning Algorithm (FERA) based on sustainability performance score (SPS) for AFSCs. FERA is an extension of the Evidential Reasoning Algorithm (ERA) with the fuzzy set theory. The criteria in this study considered are from the triple bottom line (TBL) concept of sustainability (i.e., economic (EC), environmental (EN), and social (SO)) and the SPS is calculated for all CSFs. Furthermore, to validate the reliability of the obtained results, a sensitivity analysis is also performed. The findings reveal that “technological readiness”, “technological compatibility”, and “collaboration among stakeholders” are the top three CSFs, respectively, based on the SPS value obtained after employing FERA. The key contributions are: First, it is among the earliest studies to advance the TOE framework by incorporating “resilience dimension”, for I4.0T adoption to integrate both sustainability and resilience in AFSCs. Second, by utilising the proposed extended TOE framework, we identify twenty CSFs that facilitate I4.0T adoption to integrate sustainability and resilience in AFSCs. Eventually, this study presents an integrated model to compute the SPS value, considering the TBL concept of sustainability, for identified CSFs.

Corresponding author

Abstract

The environmental impacts of apparel and textile supply chains are driving increasing stakeholder and regulatory pressures to adopt more sustainable and circular practices, particularly within the European Union. While existing research on sustainable supply chain management has predominantly focused on multinational corporations, limited attention has been given to how small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) engage with and influence sustainability transitions across multi-tier supply chains. This research investigates how small and medium-sized European apparel and textile firms incentivise suppliers to adopt and sustain circular practices across supply chain tiers. It examines how stakeholder pressures shape SME priorities and how these pressures are translated into relational governance approaches and incentive mechanisms. Drawing on Stakeholder Theory and Stewardship Theory, the study examines two case studies of European SME apparel and textile brands. The study identifies key external pressures influencing SME adoption of circular supply chain practices, as well as the relational and contractual tools used to incentivise circularity, and explores the challenges and opportunities SMEs face in implementing such practices. The research contributes empirical insights into SME circular supply chain governance and offers implications for how circular economy strategies can be operationalised within apparel and textile supply chains and other high-impact industries characterised by complex supplier networks.

A046 Track 4 Lecture Room 1 13:00-14:00

Electric Vehicle Accidents and Shareholder Value: Evidence from Firms, Suppliers, and Rivals

Presenting author
Xinrui Zhan
Corresponding author
Xinrui Zhan
Abstract

Grounded in signaling theory and supply chain risk spillover literature, this study examines how electric vehicle (EV) accidents affect focal firms, suppliers, and competitors. Using an event study and cross-sectional regression analysis, we analyze 128 EV accident events in mainland China and measure stock market reactions through abnormal returns (AR) and cumulative abnormal returns (CAR). The results show that EV accidents generate significantly negative market reactions for focal firms, with cumulative losses reaching approximately −1.6% over the [0,2] event window. Accidents involving autopilot systems trigger stronger negative responses, while accident severity do not consistently amplify market penalties. Beyond focal firms, significant upstream spillover effects are observed, as suppliers experience negative abnormal returns following accident events, particularly when they are highly dependent on the focal firm. In contrast, the impact on competitors is generally weak and short-lived, suggesting that investors interpret EV accidents primarily as industry-wide risk signals rather than opportunities for competitive gains. By demonstrating how negative operational signals propagate across supply chain networks, this study extends signaling theory and contributes to the supply chain risk literature. The findings highlight the systemic nature of operational risks in the EV industry and underscore the importance of supply chain resilience, risk diversification, and effective governance for managing accident-related uncertainties.

Corresponding author

Abstract

This study based on organizational ambidexterity theory to construct an integrated analytical framework. They provide practical implications for business managers to optimize DS-driven innovation strategies and create sustainable value. Finally, this paper also identifies the limitations of the current research and proposes avenues for future studies.

A052 Track 4 Lecture Room 1 13:00-14:00

Natural Disaster Shocks and the Mitigating Role of Subsidy Policies: Evidence from the UK Beef Supply Chain

Presenting author
Zeyao Li
Corresponding author
Zeyao Li
Abstract

This paper examines how natural-disaster shocks and subsidy policies jointly affect financial resilience, supply-side structure and market concentration in the UK domestic beef supply chain. The UK beef supply chain is characterized by multiple actors, long biological production cycles and asymmetric power between upstream and downstream stages. Farms bear feed, energy, labour and climate risks, whereas slaughtering, processing and retail actors can more easily maintain consumer-facing supply through scale and order reallocation. To capture the coexistence of downstream stability and upstream vulnerability, the paper develops an agent-based model (ABM) that includes large, medium and small farms, slaughterhouses, retailers and an external supply interface. The model evaluates scenarios with DCOH, effective capacity share, severe financial distress, PCW and slaughterhouse fulfilment. Two risk pathways are distinguished: externally transmitted cost shocks through imported inputs, energy and transport; and domestic natural-disaster shocks caused by drought or extreme heat. Results show that concentration can emerge even in the baseline. Under stronger external cost pressure, small-farm DCOH declines markedly, severe distress and market-share losses increase, while downstream fulfilment may remain high. Subsidy performance depends on the alignment between policy instrument and risk pathway. Forage-improvement subsidies relax resource-side constraints but offer limited short-term cash-flow protection when external cost pressures dominate. Cost-optimisation subsidies act more directly on expenditure pressure and therefore more consistently protect small-farm DCOH, reduce severe distress and slow structural concentration. The paper reframes food supply chain resilience from whether supply is interrupted to how supply-side structure is reorganised and provides mechanism-based evidence for targeted subsidy design in the UK domestic beef supply chain under natural-disaster risk.

A009 Track 5 Lecture Room 1 14:15-15:15

Indiscriminate or membership-based? The logistics service strategies for e-commerce platforms

Presenting author
Liangyu Geng
Corresponding author
Liangyu Geng
Abstract

Problem definition: E-commerce platforms that operate self-built logistics networks try to extend these services to external channels. In doing so, they typically adopt one of two logistics strategies: Indiscriminate Logistics Service (ILS), which provides high-level services to all consumers, and Membership-Based Logistics Service (MLS), which restricts premium services to paying members. Are platforms and manufacturers better off under ILS or MLS? Does MLS necessarily attract more members than ILS? How do membership fees, logistics service levels, and external cost factors shape the optimal strategy and the alignment of platform-manufacturer interests? Methodology: We develop a theoretical model that captures platforms’ logistics strategy choices, service level decisions, and manufacturers’ channel strategies. Results: We characterize the conditions under which ILS or MLS dominates in terms of membership attraction and joint outcomes for platforms and manufacturers. MLS does not always attract more members. When both membership fees and logistics service levels are low, ILS is more effective because price discounts dominate service upgrades in influencing consumer decisions. The optimal strategy depends on the interaction between membership fees and logistics service levels. When membership fees are high and logistics levels are moderate, platform and manufacturer incentives may diverge; otherwise, win-win outcomes can arise. Furthermore, under MLS, higher third-party logistics costs can increase membership when fees remain moderate. In contrast, under ILS, even low membership fees may fail to attract members if consumers strongly prefer direct purchasing channels. Managerial implications: Our findings provide guidance for platforms in designing logistics and membership strategies, as well as in maintaining effective and cooperative relationships with manufacturers.

A029 Track 6 Lecture Room 3 14:15-15:15

Green Bond Issuance and Supply Base Environmental Misconduct

Presenting author
Geng Wang
Corresponding author
Geng Wang
Abstract

Green bonds have become an important instrument through which firms finance environmental initiatives and signal environmental commitment. While prior research has examined the effects of green bond issuance on issuing firms and capital-market participants, little is known about whether such effects extend beyond firm boundaries to suppliers. Drawing on signaling theory, we examine how focal firm green bond issuance affects environmental misconduct within its supply base. We argue that green bond issuance makes supplier environmental misconduct more salient and consequential. After green bond issuance, focal firms may become more sensitive to environmental risks in their supply bases, while suppliers may expect environmental misconduct to be more likely to be detected, more costly, and more damaging to future exchange with the focal firm. We further argue that this effect is strengthened by supply base relationship duration, which makes the signal clearer, but weakened by supply base competition, which may increase cost pressure and reduce suppliers’ willingness or ability to make environmental adjustments. Using a buyer-year panel that combines green bond issuance, buyer–supplier relationships, supplier environmental misconduct, and financial data, we estimate difference-in-differences models with buyer and year fixed effects. The results show that green bond issuance is associated with a significant reduction in environmental misconduct within focal firms’ supply bases, corresponding to an approximately 15.6% reduction at the sample mean. Consistent with our theorizing, the effect is stronger when supply base relationship duration is longer and weaker when supply base competition is higher. Event-study and robustness tests support the validity of the findings, and additional tests suggest that the effect is unlikely to be driven by supply base restructuring. This study extends green bond research by documenting supply base spillover effects and contributes to sustainable supply chain research by identifying capital-market environmental signals as an antecedent of supplier environmental behavior.

A057 Track 5 Lecture Room 1 14:15-15:15

Taming the Surge: Adaptive Inventory Control for Live-Stream E-commerce under Impulse Buying

Presenting author
zhuoqun li
Corresponding author
zhuoqun li
Abstract

Live-stream e-commerce has grown rapidly, but its unique features—impulse buying and high return rates—create significant challenges for inventory management. Demand surges unpredictably, and return rates as high as 30–40% constantly change the available stock. Traditional inventory models, which assume stable and predictable demand, fail to handle these dynamics effectively. To address this problem, we develop a behavioral inventory model based on the S-O-R (Stimulus-Organism-Response) framework. We identify three key live-stream stimuli—anchor attractiveness, marketing intensity, and situational pressure—and embed them into a system dynamics model to capture the causal relationships from marketing decisions to impulse buying and ultimately to ordering behavior. We then propose an adaptive ordering strategy that adjusts its forecast, inventory, and pipeline coefficients based on impulse buying intention and stockout risk. Using real sales data from a women's apparel live-stream business, we simulate two demand scenarios: pulse-type surges and regular fluctuations. Results show that our adaptive strategy consistently outperforms the traditional model, achieving lower total supply chain costs, faster recovery after demand spikes, and better stability under normal operations. Our findings suggest that incorporating consumer behavior into inventory decisions offers a practical and effective way to manage supply chains in the live-stream e-commerce environment.

Investor Optimism, Public Skepticism: Divergent Stakeholder Responses to Carbon Neutrality Achievement and Emissions Trading

Presenting author
Abraham Zhang
Corresponding author
Abraham Zhang
Abstract

Purpose Following the Paris Agreement in 2015, many firms pursued carbon neutrality, with some ‘early movers’ successfully achieving this milestone. This study examines the financial and public implications of carbon neutrality achievement (CNA) in the United States, analyzing stock market and Twitter reactions and the moderating role of emissions trading. Design/methodology/approach We analyze 104 CNA announcements from 2016 to 2023 using event study methodology and a propensity-score-matching and difference-in-difference (PSM-DiD) approach to assess long-run operating performance. Public reactions are measured via Twitter sentiment and volume, covering over 75.8 million tweets. Findings CNA announcements generate positive stock market reactions (p < 5%) in Day (-1, 1). In contrast, public reactions are largely negative, with nearly three-quarters of announcements eliciting overall negative sentiment in Day 1. A positive effect (p < 5%) of CNA announcements on Twitter volume in Day 0 and Day 1 highlights the relevance and importance of this study. Further analysis shows that CNAs improve long-term sales performance (p < 1%). Weak evidence suggests that Twitter sentiment positively affects stock market reactions (p < 10%), indicating that investors partially consider public perceptions. Emissions trading strengthens investor reactions (p < 10%) but amplifies negative public sentiment (p < 10%), reflecting its controversial nature—financially beneficial to investors but viewed skeptically by the public. Relevance/contribution This study is original in focusing on carbon neutrality “achievement”, rather than “intentions” or “commitments”, providing empirical evidence on both financial and reputational impacts. Findings highlight the contrasting reactions of investors and the public and suggest that firms should complement carbon neutrality initiatives with strategic social media management, transparent communication, and proactive stakeholder engagement to maximize financial performance while mitigating public skepticism.

El Nino Shocks and Global Supply-Chain Adaptation

Presenting author
Kun Tian
Corresponding author
Kun Tian
Abstract

This paper examines whether El Niño shocks trigger supply-chain reconfiguration through a concentration-first crisis response followed by diversification-led adaptation. Using NOAA Oceanic Niño Index data matched to monthly bilateral import flows from UN Comtrade, I estimate an importer-sector-month panel covering 10 countries and 7 climate-sensitive agricultural commodity groups over 2010–2024. Two-way fixed-effects regressions with shift-share identification reveal that El Niño episodes significantly raise sourcing concentration (HHI +0.036, p = 0.030) and import volatility (+0.112, p = 0.025) in exposed supply chains. Dynamic local projections document a delayed reversal: over 6–12 months, exposed sectors progressively reduce concentration and widen supplier networks, consistent with purposive resilience investment following revealed disruption risk. The paper contributes a two-phase adaptation model and novel causal evidence on how recurring climate uncertainty reshapes global sourcing architecture.

Natural disaster and firm performance: A multi-tier supply chain perspective

Presenting author
Jingcong Xu
Corresponding author
Hangfei Guo
Abstract

Firms have noticed that natural disasters in their supply chains pose significant risks to their operations. Existing research has predominantly examined the effects of natural disasters that directly impact firms' operations. This study extends the literature by adopting a multi-tier supply chain perspective to empirically examine the impact of natural disasters affecting a firm’s Tier-2 suppliers on its operating performance, and how this impact is influenced by the resilience of its Tier-1 suppliers. We construct an 11-year panel comprising more than 13,000 supply chains of UK-listed companies. The results reveal that natural disasters affecting Tier-2 suppliers significantly deteriorate UK firms’ operating performance, providing empirical evidence that such disruptions propagate through multi-tier supply chains. This adverse effect is significantly mitigated when Tier-1 suppliers exhibit strong production resilience, whereas Tier-1 suppliers’ inventory slack does not moderate it. This finding contributes to the literature by revealing that productivity and inventory slack, two widely used strategies for building supply chain resilience, play distinct roles in mitigating the negative impacts of natural disasters affecting supply chains. It also provides empirical evidence of the ripple effects of natural disasters in multi-tier supply chains. We suggest that UK firms adopt a multi-tier supply chain perspective to manage natural disaster risks and collaborate with supply chain partners to build resilience. Keywords: Natural disaster; Multi-tier supply chain; Resilience; UK; Ripple effect

A032 Track 3 Lecture Room 3 15:40-17:40

Countering the “Double Squeeze” in Agri-food Supply Chains: The Role of Smallholder Farmer Cooperatives

Presenting author
Ghadafi Razak
Corresponding author
Ghadafi Razak
Abstract

The prevalence of power imbalances in the upstream of agri-food supply chains often places smallholder farmers in a “double squeeze”: farmers depend on powerful upstream actors such as input suppliers, state agencies, and certification bodies for access to seeds, fertiliser, and technical support, while downstream power is held by buyers, processors, and exporters that control market access, price formation, and sustainability premiums. Cooperatives provide a unique model that serves as an effective mechanism for smallholder farmers to mobilise, empower, and convert individual-level risks into collective risks. Yet, how such collectives operate, gain legitimacy, and influence sustainability outcomes under persistent power asymmetry remains under-theorised. This study draws on qualitative data collected via semi-structured interviews (35) and two focus group discussions with key stakeholders in Ghana’s cocoa supply chain to understand how various farmer collectives mitigate power asymmetry and advance social sustainability in AFSC upstream tiers. The data collected were triangulated and analysed abductively to guarantee reliability and validity. The findings show that farmer collectives serve as indigenous production and governance systems required to navigate structural constraints in AFSCs by: (1) formalising cooperation with strong community legitimacy; (2) pooling resources to improve resilience; and (3) enabling farmer voice and learning. The analysis suggests that collective decision-making processes, social norms and expectations, informal authority structures, and conflict management constitute an informal governance that substitutes for formal institutional structures. While collectives enhance farmer coordination, they also reveal tensions around representation and voice. Overall, these farmer collectives enable sustainability to cascade horizontally across farmers via knowledge sharing and peer learning, collective enforcement of practices, and diversification of livelihoods and income sources. The study conceptualises how indigenous collective organising functions as a form of countervailing governance under power asymmetry. For managers, policymakers, and development actors, the study highlights the limitations of top-down, compliance-driven sustainability interventions.

When Banks Compete, Firms Speak: Evidence from Supply Chain Disclosure

Presenting author
Morris Ming Liu
Corresponding author
Morris Ming Liu
Abstract

This study explores how bank competition, delineated by the regional distribution of banking branches, influences firm-level supply chain disclosure. With a sample of Chinese listed non-financial firms ranging from 2007 to 2022, this study finds that heightened competition among banks leads to firms’ increasing disclosure of their supply chain. Global Supply Chain Network Configuration and Supply Chain Resilience: Evidence from Chinese Equipment Manufacturing Industry

Presenting author
Jiehui Yang
Corresponding author
Jiehui Yang
Abstract

Amid escalating geopolitical frictions and supply chain disruptions, firms are restructuring their global networks, yet the nuanced effects of spatial–structural configurations on resilience remain theoretically ambiguous. Drawing on open innovation and institutional theories, this study disentangles the heterogeneous impacts of globalization depth and breadth across supplier, customer, and internal subnetworks on supply chain resilience, and proposes a dual mediation mechanism through technological innovation and supply chain power. Using panel data from Chinese listed equipment manufacturers and employing fixed-effects and mediation models, we find that depth and breadth of supplier and customer networks significantly enhance resilience, whereas both dimensions of internal networks exhibit negative effects. Mechanism tests reveal that supplier depth, customer depth, and customer breadth promote resilience via stimulating technological innovation, while internal depth impairs resilience through innovation suppression. Concurrently, supplier breadth and customer depth strengthen resilience by enhancing supply chain power, yet internal breadth erodes resilience by weakening relative power. This research advances supply chain resilience theory by unpacking the differential roles of global subnetwork configurations and uncovering a dual-path transmission logic, offering actionable guidance for designing resilient global supply chains in volatile environments.

Corresponding author

Abstract

Cumulative innovation experience is widely recognized as a driver of mean innovation quality, yet whether experience influences the consistency of innovation quality remains largely unexplored.
